Moons of the Solar System

Description:

Moons, also known as natural satellites, are celestial bodies that orbit planets and dwarf planets. The Solar System contains over 290 confirmed moons, ranging in size from small asteroid-like bodies to massive worlds with atmospheres, oceans, and geological activity. Each planet (except Mercury and Venus) has at least one moon, and some have dozens.

Moons vary greatly in composition and origin. Some formed from the same material as their parent planet, while others are likely captured asteroids or formed from collision debris. The diversity of moons provides valuable insights into the history and evolution of the Solar System.

Interesting Facts:

  • Ganymede (Jupiter's moon) is the largest moon in the Solar System — even bigger than Mercury.
  • Titan (Saturn’s moon) has a dense atmosphere and rivers of methane.
  • Europa (Jupiter’s moon) may have a subsurface ocean that could harbor life.
  • Io (Jupiter’s moon) is the most volcanically active body in the Solar System.
  • Enceladus (Saturn’s moon) shoots water-ice geysers from its south pole.
